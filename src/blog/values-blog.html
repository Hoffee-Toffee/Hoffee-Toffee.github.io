An ethical decision

Probably the longest blog I have done, it took me quite a while of writing and editing to get it to a point that I am comfortable with sharing.
This was about the only real impactful ethical decision that I have made.
The story below has been heavily sanitized.

I was in a group project with four friends.
After divvying up the tasks and initial planning, things went off to a pretty good start.
I focused on getting my share of the project done, but it wasn't long before the other four people began having disputes over a certain aspect of the project.

At this point, the project was pretty fleshed out, almost 3 months in.
Things started to get fairly heated and I was put in a very uncomfortable situation where each side tried to get me to back them.
The dispute was split with 2 on each side, it started with each side attempting to explain and convince everyone else of their plan.
Both sides became fixed, but I remained neutral, considering and responding to each point on merit alone.

As time went on, this led to each side wanting me to help pressure the other side into giving up.
And then it led to each side wanting to continue the project without the others.
However, both wanted to have the majority to have an advantage, so I was fought over.

The project was at a complete standstill at this point, all I wanted was to continue work.
I again remained neutral, as each side was now being very petty, I didn't want anything to do with it.
At this point, I was praised by a side whenever I agreed with their point and berated when I didn't.

I had to leave the project at this time because of the stress.
It didn't help that I really enjoyed the work - when we were actually working - and I had to give that up, I missed it a lot.

I'm not sure exactly what happened during the next month or so, but another member eventually left for what I assume to be the same reason that I did.
The other person on their side was basically forced to leave also, and after a while the remaining two invited me back.
The two that left remain very bitter after the ordeal, which isn't surprising, given the situation.

It took me a fair while to decide that I would rejoin.
I was never happy about doing so, It'd already soured two friendships, so I decided that I would not lose the project too.

I wish I had had the confidence and skills to resolve the conflict, without it ending as it did.

The project was never the same again, and it ended up costing two good friendships.

Influences

Grew up in a pretty divided family, parents each having incompatable views on life.
That kind of left me not knowing what to think, which was pretty hard, but I think it had some positives.
It did mean that I didn't have any strong beliefs from a young age, anything I would accept as true at an age before I could truly think things through.
The difference in views always left me feeling like a bit of an outsider, probably a factor in my social anxiety and low confidence.
I generally do fine in everyday activities, but not so much if it's anything personal or new, which is to be expected really.

Strengths

I have many years of programming experience, just not in industry.
I also have a passion for it, which will be helpful.

Limitations

As mentioned previously, I have little confidence, as well as little self-esteem.

Stratagies

Well, going off the group project incident described above, there is a strategy that I do try to use.
It probably came out of thinking for programming, but I now often do it with plot in stories also.

This is by simply outlining all the properties a solution must have, including anything ruled out.
Using this and other data available, I can build on this and eventually get to a solution, or occasionally find a clash which means it can't work (unless another element is changed to resolve the clash).
It's something I've been trying to build into a habit.

In the group project, I used this strategy myself, but it was never adopted by the rest of the group and did very little in the end.
